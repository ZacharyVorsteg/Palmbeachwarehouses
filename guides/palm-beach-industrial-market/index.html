Palm Beach County Industrial Market Report 2026: Trends, Rates & Outlook

By Zachary Vorsteg • February 27, 2026 • 18 min read

The Palm Beach County industrial market is in a tight, supply-constrained state that favors well-positioned landlords and makes strategic site selection critical for tenants. This report provides you with a comprehensive snapshot of the market as of early 2026: where prices stand, where development is happening, what's driving demand, and what you should expect in the coming 12-24 months.

Whether you're a tenant looking to lease space, an investor evaluating acquisitions, or a business owner planning expansion, understanding the market dynamics is essential to making informed decisions. Let's dive into the data.

Market Overview: The Palm Beach County Industrial Landscape

Palm Beach County's industrial real estate market encompasses approximately 150+ million square feet of warehouse, distribution, manufacturing, and flex space across the county. The market is divided into multiple distinct submarkets, each with its own characteristics, pricing, and demand drivers.

Key Market Statistics (As of February 2026)

  • Total inventory: Approximately 150-160 million square feet across all property classes
  • Vacancy rate: 3-5% countywide, with significant variation by submarket
  • Average rent: $12-18/SF NNN annually (varies significantly by location and building class)
  • Market absorption: Positive net absorption—more space being leased than becoming available
  • New construction: 2-4 million SF in planning or early development phases countywide
  • Investor activity: Strong acquisition interest, cap rates 4.5-6.5% depending on location

Bottom line: This is a landlord's market. Tenants face tight supply, limited negotiating leverage, and rising rents. Planning ahead is critical; waiting until you need space means limited options and higher costs.

Submarket Breakdown: Where Industrial Space Exists in Palm Beach County

Industrial space is not evenly distributed across the county. Some submarkets are mature and fully developed. Others are emerging. Understanding each submarket's characteristics is essential to finding the right location for your business.

West Palm Beach Industrial Corridor (Southern Boulevard to I-95)

The West Palm Beach corridor is the most developed and active industrial submarket in Palm Beach County. It stretches along Southern Boulevard, I-95, and Northlake Boulevard. This is where the majority of industrial activity, investment, and new construction occurs.

Characteristics:

  • Prime location: Direct I-95 access, close to Port of Palm Beach, central to the county
  • Rent range: $14-20/SF NNN (premium pricing)
  • Vacancy: 2-4% (tight)
  • Building class: Mix of older 14-foot warehouses and newer 18-20 foot modern spaces
  • Tenant mix: Distribution, logistics, e-commerce fulfillment, light manufacturing
  • Development activity: Highest in county; multiple speculative projects underway

Best for: Tenants requiring highway access, port proximity, and willing to pay premium pricing. Large distribution operations, logistics companies, parcel carriers.

Examples of submarkets within WPB corridor: Westgate industrial park, Commerce Parks, Northlake Boulevard industrial area.

Southern Boulevard / Port-Adjacent Market

This submarket extends from West Palm Beach south toward the Port of Palm Beach and includes properties within 2-3 miles of the port. Port proximity is a major value driver.

Characteristics:

  • Port access: Critical differentiator—within 5-10 minutes of Port of Palm Beach
  • Rent range: $13-19/SF NNN (premium for port proximity)
  • Vacancy: 2-4% (tight)
  • Building age: Mix of older and newer buildings
  • Tenant focus: Heavy logistics, import/export, international freight handlers
  • Infrastructure: Port-related tenants, trucking companies, consolidation facilities

Best for: Import/export businesses, freight forwarders, consolidation centers, businesses with frequent port interaction.

Northlake Boulevard / Interstate Access

Northlake Boulevard runs east-west and provides excellent access to both I-95 and the turnpike. Industrial properties along Northlake have good highway connectivity.

Characteristics:

  • Highway access: I-95 north, turnpike east, good regional connectivity
  • Rent range: $12-18/SF NNN
  • Vacancy: 3-5%
  • Building profile: Standard industrial, mostly 14-18 feet clear height
  • Tenant mix: Distribution, manufacturing, flex space, office-warehouse

Best for: Mid-range tenants seeking good highway access without paying downtown premiums. Regional distributors, light manufacturing, flex operators.

Boca Raton Industrial Market

Boca Raton has a small but significant industrial footprint, primarily in west Boca. The submarket is less congested than West Palm Beach but offers good highway access.

Characteristics:

  • Size: Smaller submarket than West Palm; limited available properties
  • Rent range: $12-17/SF NNN
  • Vacancy: 3-6% (slightly higher than WPB, more inventory options)
  • Location: Scattered properties west of Federal Highway and near I-95
  • Tenant mix: Mix of industrial, flex, light manufacturing, and office-warehouse
  • Demographics: Affluent market; less price-sensitive tenants

Best for: High-end light manufacturing, design-focused flex space, businesses serving Boca's affluent market, operations requiring upscale surroundings.

Delray Beach Industrial Area

Delray Beach has growing industrial inventory, particularly in the western industrial park areas. The submarket is more price-competitive than Boca or downtown West Palm Beach.

Characteristics:

  • Market position: Secondary market, more affordable than premium WPB locations
  • Rent range: $11-16/SF NNN
  • Vacancy: 4-6% (more availability than WPB)
  • Development: Expanding industrial inventory with new projects
  • Tenant focus: Regional distribution, light manufacturing, flex space, value-conscious tenants
  • Access: Good highway access via I-95 and Atlantic Avenue

Best for: Price-sensitive tenants, growing companies seeking to control costs, regional operations, light manufacturing.

Boynton Beach Industrial Market

Boynton Beach represents the southern boundary of significant industrial development. The submarket includes newer development around the Highway A1A and I-95 interchange.

Characteristics:

  • Market tier: Value market, less expensive than central county
  • Rent range: $10-15/SF NNN
  • Vacancy: 4-7% (relatively more available space)
  • Building class: Mostly standard industrial, some newer spaces
  • Tenant profile: Regional distributors, light manufacturing, storage operations
  • Growth trajectory: Improving as development pushes south

Best for: Cost-conscious tenants, regional operations with less demand for premium location, light manufacturing, distribution.

Jupiter / Northern County Market

Jupiter represents the northern edge of the county's developed industrial market. Industrial space is limited but growing.

Characteristics:

  • Market role: Emerging submarket, limited but growing inventory
  • Rent range: $11-17/SF NNN
  • Vacancy: 5-8% (higher availability due to smaller overall inventory)
  • Location focus: Concentrated near Indiantown Road and I-95
  • Development momentum: Increasing as northern growth corridor expands
  • Tenant types: Regional distribution, light manufacturing, flex space

Best for: Operations serving Palm Beach County north/central regions, businesses willing to trade central location for lower cost, growth-oriented tenants.

Lake Worth, Riviera Beach, Royal Palm Beach, Lantana

These secondary/tertiary submarkets include scattered industrial properties, generally offering better value than central county but with less developed infrastructure.

Characteristics:

  • Rent range: $9-14/SF NNN (most affordable in county)
  • Vacancy: 5-8%
  • Availability: Limited inventory; these are smaller markets
  • Tenant profile: Local operators, value-conscious, less sophisticated logistics needs
  • Infrastructure: Less developed than premium markets; may require more operational flexibility

Best for: Local operators, small businesses, cost-minimization focus, regional rather than national distribution.

Important note on NNN (triple net) pricing: These are base rents. You pay separately for property taxes (varies by location, typically $1-3/SF annually), insurance ($0.25-0.75/SF), and CAM—common area maintenance ($0.50-1.50/SF). True occupancy cost is base rent + these additional expenses.

Vacancy Trends and Available Inventory

Palm Beach County's industrial market has experienced steady tightening over the past 18-24 months. Vacancy rates have moved from 5-6% to 3-5% as absorption outpaces new supply.

Historical Vacancy Context

  • 2023: 5-6% vacancy (recovering from pandemic dislocation)
  • 2024: 4-5% vacancy (steady tightening)
  • Early 2026: 3-5% vacancy (tight market)

This tightening has several consequences for the market:

  • For tenants: Less choice, less negotiating leverage, pressure to accept available terms, need to plan ahead (6-12 months in advance)
  • For landlords: Strong rental pricing power, ability to select tenants, less concern about extended vacant periods
  • For investors: Strong fundamentals, positive absorption, pricing supports acquisition values, investment appetite high

New Construction and Development Pipeline

Despite tight supply, new development is occurring, particularly in West Palm Beach and emerging markets. However, construction costs remain elevated, limiting the pace of new supply.

Active Projects (2026)

Approximately 2-4 million square feet of industrial space is in planning, permitting, or early construction phases county-wide. Projects are distributed across multiple submarkets:

  • West Palm Beach corridor: Majority of new construction; multiple 100,000-500,000 SF projects under development
  • Southern Boulevard: Smaller project activity; new buildings typically 50,000-150,000 SF
  • Delray Beach/Boynton: Growing development activity; mix of sizes
  • Other submarkets: Limited new construction, mostly infill/renovation projects

New Construction Pricing

New buildings command significant premiums over existing stock (typically 10-20% higher rents) because they offer:

  • 18-20+ feet clear height vs. 14 feet in older buildings
  • Modern dock equipment and configurations
  • Better HVAC and climate control systems
  • Efficient column spacing (30-40 feet vs. 20 feet in older buildings)
  • Longer building lifespan (50-year vs. potential facility obsolescence)
  • Energy efficiency features
  • Modern security and fire protection systems

Strategic consideration: For growing operations, the 10-20% premium for new construction often pays for itself through increased operational efficiency, lower maintenance costs, and flexibility for reconfiguration. Don't automatically assume older, cheaper space is the better deal—total cost of occupancy matters more than headline rent.

Demand Drivers: Why Industrial Space Is Tight in Palm Beach County

E-Commerce Growth and Last-Mile Delivery

E-commerce penetration continues to grow (20%+ of retail nationally), driving demand for fulfillment centers and last-mile distribution hubs. South Florida, with its population density and proximity to Caribbean/Latin America markets, is a natural hub for e-commerce operations. This structural demand supports tight market conditions.

Population Growth and Local Consumption

Palm Beach County's population grows approximately 2-3% annually, above the national average. Growing population drives consumption, which requires distribution infrastructure. This demographic tailwind supports sustained industrial demand.

Nearshoring and Reshoring Trends

Companies are shifting sourcing from distant overseas suppliers back to North America to reduce supply chain risk and complexity. South Florida, with its proximity to Caribbean and Latin American production, is a natural hub for nearshoring. This is a multi-year trend that supports industrial space demand.

Port of Palm Beach and Logistics Infrastructure

The Port of Palm Beach handles 10+ million tons annually and is undergoing expansion. Port-related logistics employment and space demand remain strong. Properties within 2-3 miles of the port command premiums and remain in tight supply.

Regional Distribution Hub Status

South Florida serves as a distribution hub for the southeastern US, Caribbean, and Latin America. This transportation and logistics function requires extensive warehouse space. The position is structural and durable.

Florida's Business Environment

No state income tax, business-friendly regulations, and good weather attract businesses and support economic growth. This creates positive long-term demand for industrial space and real estate in general.

Tenant Mix: Who Leases Industrial Space in Palm Beach County?

Understanding tenant types helps predict future demand and market stability:

Distribution and Logistics (40% of market)

Large distribution companies (Amazon, UPS, FedEx, XPO Logistics, etc.) and regional distributors lease the most industrial space. This segment remains strong and is expected to grow.

E-Commerce Fulfillment (25% of market)

Direct-to-consumer e-commerce companies lease fulfillment centers. This segment experienced rapid growth 2020-2023 and has stabilized. Continued growth expected as e-commerce penetration increases.

Light Manufacturing and Assembly (15% of market)

Companies that manufacture or assemble products (furniture, medical devices, food production, etc.) lease warehouse/manufacturing hybrid spaces. This segment is stable with gradual growth.

Cold Storage and Temperature-Controlled (10% of market)

Refrigerated and frozen food storage, pharmaceutical storage, and other temperature-sensitive operations. This niche is growing due to increased food import/export and pharmaceutical distribution.

Flex Space and Office-Warehouse (10% of market)

Mixed office and warehouse space for smaller companies, contractors, and service businesses. This segment is stable.

Investment Market Perspective: Cap Rates and Asset Values

For investors considering industrial property acquisition in Palm Beach County:

Current Cap Rates

  • Class A properties (West Palm Beach, port-adjacent): 4.5-5.5% (prime assets command lowest yields)
  • Class A- properties (Northlake, secondary premium): 5.0-5.75%
  • Class B+ properties (Delray, Boynton, value markets): 5.5-6.5%
  • Class B properties (secondary/tertiary markets): 6.0-7.0%

Sale Price Ranges

  • Class A newer buildings: $120-150+/SF (8-10 year old, well-located, long-term tenants)
  • Class A standard buildings: $80-120/SF (stable 14-18 foot space, good location)
  • Class B+ value properties: $65-90/SF (secondary location, standard condition)
  • Class B secondary properties: $50-75/SF (value markets, older buildings, smaller properties)

Investment Drivers

Investors are attracted to Palm Beach County industrial due to:

  • Strong fundamentals (tight vacancy, positive absorption)
  • Logistics hub positioning and port proximity
  • Population growth and demographic tailwinds
  • Net lease structures (tenant pays operating expenses)
  • Long-term tenant leases (5-10 year terms)
  • Strong regional economic growth

Comparison to Broward and Miami-Dade Markets

Understanding how Palm Beach County stacks up against neighboring industrial markets is important for strategic positioning:

Miami-Dade Industrial Market

Characteristics: Smaller total inventory, tighter vacancy (2-3%), higher rents ($15-22/SF), limited new supply, older building stock, heavy port and international trade focus.

Advantages: Port proximity, international trade gateway, established logistics ecosystem. Disadvantages: Congestion, higher costs, limited modern buildings.

Comparison to Palm Beach: Miami-Dade is more expensive and congested. Palm Beach offers more available space, better value, and growing modern construction. Miami-Dade remains superior for intensive port/import-export operations; Palm Beach better for growing companies seeking value.

Broward Industrial Market

Characteristics: Mature market, heavily developed, vacancy 3-4%, rents $13-19/SF, limited new development (land constraints), established tenant base.

Advantages: Established market, good infrastructure, mature tenant ecosystem. Disadvantages: Limited growth potential, low new supply, limited options.

Comparison to Palm Beach: Broward is fully developed with few available sites. Palm Beach has more growth potential, more available land, more new construction, and better value. Broward's advantage is maturity and established infrastructure; Palm Beach is the growth market.

Strategic Market Positioning

Palm Beach County increasingly positions itself as the value alternative to Miami-Dade while avoiding Broward's full development. For tenants seeking port proximity with better pricing and availability, Palm Beach is increasingly attractive. For investors seeking growth markets with strong fundamentals, Palm Beach offers better opportunities than fully-developed Broward.

2026-2027 Outlook and Market Predictions

Lease Rate Expectations

Prediction: Continued modest rent growth (2-4% annually) across most submarkets. Premium locations (West Palm Beach, port-adjacent) may see 3-5% growth. Secondary markets may see 1-3% growth as new supply comes online.

Rationale: Tight supply supports landlord pricing power, but new construction will provide some relief in secondary markets. Overall inflationary environment and operating cost increases will pressure rents upward.

Vacancy and Supply Expectations

Prediction: Vacancy rates may tick up slightly (to 4-6% range) as new supply comes online in 2026-2027. However, strong demand will keep the market relatively tight overall.

Rationale: 2-4M SF of new construction will add supply, but e-commerce growth, population growth, and logistics demand should absorb much of it. Market will remain tenant-cautious but less extreme than current conditions.

New Construction Activity

Prediction: Increased new development, primarily in West Palm Beach and emerging southern markets. 3-5M SF of new supply in development pipeline.

Rationale: Rents support new development; available land exists; investor appetite is strong. Construction cost inflation will slow some projects, but the fundamentals support continued activity.

Tenant Mix Evolution

Prediction: E-commerce growth moderates, but continues. Last-mile delivery hubs expand. Cold storage and specialty/flex space grow. Light manufacturing remains stable.

Rationale: E-commerce penetration is slowing growth rate but remains structural. Regional delivery hub demand remains strong. Specialty spaces (cold storage, flex) are high-margin for landlords.

Investment Market Perspective

Prediction: Strong institutional investor interest continues. Cap rates may compress slightly (0.25-0.5%) as strong demand supports values. Foreign investment may increase if capital flows to US real estate improve.

Rationale: Industrial fundamentals remain strong. Yields are attractive relative to other asset classes. Limited development land supports long-term value. Risk: economic slowdown or recession would pressure demand and rents.

Biggest Risks to Outlook

  • Economic recession: Would reduce consumer spending and e-commerce demand, pressuring rents and vacancies upward
  • Interest rate environment: Higher rates reduce investor returns and slow development activity
  • Supply chain normalization: If supply chain costs return to normal, nearshoring demand could soften
  • Oversupply in secondary markets: Too much new construction in secondary markets could pressure rents
  • Labor market tightness: Continued difficulty attracting warehouse workers could constrain operations and demand

Strategic Recommendations for Tenants, Owners, and Investors

For Tenants Leasing Space

  • Plan ahead: Plan space needs 12+ months in advance. Tight market means limited options.
  • Get professional help: Work with a broker familiar with each submarket. Local knowledge is valuable.
  • Consider value markets: Premium locations aren't always worth premium pricing. Delray, Boynton, and secondary markets offer value.
  • Verify operating expenses: Total occupancy cost (rent + NNN + utilities) matters more than headline rent.
  • Evaluate growing spaces: New construction commands premiums but offers operational efficiency that often pays for itself.

For Property Owners

  • Consider renovation: Updating older buildings to 16-18 foot clear height and modern docks can justify 15-20% rent increases.
  • Tenant quality: In tight market, you can be selective. Choose stable, long-term tenants over short-term renters.
  • Market conditions favorable: This is a good time for rent resets during lease renewals.
  • Capital investment ROI: Building improvements generate strong returns given tight market and pricing power.

For Investors

  • Growth markets offer opportunity: Emerging markets (Delray, Boynton, Jupiter) offer higher cap rates and growth potential vs. saturated Broward.
  • New construction with anchor tenants: Reduced lease-up risk if pre-leased; higher prices but lower risk.
  • Value-add opportunities: Older buildings with deferred maintenance, vacancy, or operational issues offer improvement potential.
  • Long-term hold perspective: Market fundamentals are solid; assume 7-10 year hold; strong annual cash flow and appreciation potential.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: What is the current vacancy rate in Palm Beach County industrial market?
As of early 2026, vacancy rates range from 3-5% countywide, which is considered a tight market with low availability. Premium submarkets like West Palm Beach and Southern Boulevard have tighter vacancy (2-4%), while secondary locations may have slightly higher availability (4-6%). This low vacancy environment means landlords have strong pricing power and tenants face limited negotiating leverage.
Q: What are current warehouse lease rates in Palm Beach County?
Average NNN (triple net) lease rates range from $12-18/SF annually. Premium submarkets command $14-20/SF. Secondary locations rent for $11-15/SF. Important: these are base rents. Add property taxes ($1-3/SF), insurance ($0.25-0.75/SF), and CAM ($0.50-1.50/SF) for true occupancy costs. Modern buildings (18-20 feet clear height) command 10-20% premiums over older 14-foot buildings.
Q: Which Palm Beach County industrial submarkets are most active?
The most active markets are: West Palm Beach industrial corridor (highest volume, premium pricing), Southern Boulevard (port proximity), Northlake Boulevard (highway access), and Delray Beach (value alternative). Secondary markets include Boca Raton, Boynton Beach, Jupiter, Lake Worth, and Lantana. Each submarket serves different tenant profiles and budget constraints.
Q: What is driving industrial market demand in Palm Beach County?
Key drivers include: e-commerce growth and last-mile delivery expansion, population growth (2-3% annually), nearshoring trends (supply chain reshoring to North America), Port of Palm Beach logistics infrastructure, Florida's tax-friendly business environment, and South Florida's position as a regional distribution hub. These are structural, long-term demand drivers that support the market beyond short-term cycles.
Q: How much new industrial construction is planned in Palm Beach County?
Approximately 2-4 million square feet of industrial space is in planning, permitting, or early construction phases. Majority is in West Palm Beach; additional development in Delray, Boynton, and emerging markets. New construction takes 18-24 months from groundbreaking to occupancy, so 2026-2027 is expected to see increased new supply availability, particularly in secondary markets.
Q: How does Palm Beach County compare to Broward and Miami-Dade industrial markets?
Miami-Dade is more expensive ($15-22/SF) and congested; better for intensive port operations. Broward is fully developed with limited new supply; more expensive than Palm Beach. Palm Beach offers better value, more available land, growing modern construction, and more opportunity for growth-stage companies. For tenants seeking port proximity without Miami-Dade's congestion or costs, Palm Beach is increasingly attractive.
