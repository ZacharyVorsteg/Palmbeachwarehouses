How to Lease, Buy, or Develop a Large Warehouse (10,000+ SF) in Palm Beach County

Written by: Zachary Vorsteg, Cornerstone Realty
Published: June 27, 2026
Reading Time: 14 minutes

If you're looking for industrial space in Palm Beach County and your requirements exceed 10,000 square feet, you're making a major operational decision. A large warehouse lease or purchase can represent millions of dollars in total occupancy costs over its lifetime—and the decisions you make now will impact your business for years.

This guide covers everything you need to know: when to lease versus buy versus develop, where the best submarkets are, what total occupancy costs actually look like, and most importantly, how to access off-market inventory and negotiate terms that work for your business.

Large Warehouse Decision Matrix: Lease, Buy, or Develop?

The choice between leasing, buying, or developing large industrial space depends on your capital availability, operational timeline, and long-term strategy. Here's how to think about each:

Leasing (10,000+ SF)

Leasing is the most flexible option. You preserve capital for operations, avoid long-term capital commitments, and can relocate if your business changes. Leases typically run 5-10 years, with renewal options.

Best for: Growing businesses testing new markets, companies with uncertain long-term space needs, operations that may scale up or down, or those that need flexibility for future relocation.

Buying (10,000+ SF)

Purchasing industrial property requires significant capital upfront but builds equity over time. You'll mortgage it over 15-20 years, paying principal plus interest, property taxes, insurance, and maintenance.

The advantage: you own the asset. If property values rise, you benefit. You control renovations and maintenance. You're not subject to landlord lease renewal gambles.

Best for: Established businesses planning 10+ year operations, companies wanting to build balance sheet equity, those with access to capital financing, or situations where the building itself is a strategic asset.

Developing (Land or Existing Building)

Developing occurs when you either own land and build a new warehouse, or acquire a building and significantly reposition it. Development timelines are 18-36 months from land to occupancy. Capital requirements are substantial (hard costs, soft costs, financing), and market risk is real.

Best for: Long-term operators with capital and strong market conviction, developers building for third-party tenants, or situations where you've identified severe supply/demand imbalances.

Quick math on lease vs. buy: If you plan to occupy space for fewer than 8 years, leasing is almost always cheaper. If 10+ years, buying often wins due to equity buildup and long-term capital efficiency. The crossover point depends heavily on financing terms, property values, and occupancy certainty. Always model both scenarios.

Palm Beach County Industrial Submarkets for Large Warehouses

Palm Beach County has distinct industrial submarkets, each with different rent levels, availability, and accessibility. For large warehouses (10,000+ SF), location matters intensely. Here's where to look:

West Palm Beach (Logistics Hub)

West Palm Beach is the dominant submarket for large industrial space. Proximity to I-95 and major distribution networks makes it the default choice for logistics, manufacturing, and warehousing tenants.

Characteristics: Mixed vintage with older industrial parks and newer class A buildings. Multi-tenant and single-tenant buildings available. Heavy truck traffic; 24/7 operations common.

Submarkets within WPB: Westwood/Okeechobee corridor (central), Northlake (southern WPB)

Riviera Beach & Mangonia Park (Port Access)

These areas offer direct access to the Port of Palm Beach, critical for import/export operations. Less supply than West Palm Beach, but premium for port-dependent tenants.

Characteristics: Older portfolio with limited modern space. High demand from cargo handlers, maritime services, and distribution. Port-access premium applies to rental rates.

Lake Worth & Lantana (Emerging Supply)

Mid-county, somewhat equidistant from Miami and West Palm Beach. Newer buildings coming online; less congested than WPB. Good for tenants balancing Miami/WPB accessibility.

Characteristics: Newer class A space available. Less truck-heavy than WPB; emerging supply adds flexibility for large tenants.

Boynton Beach (Mid-County Alternative)

South of Lake Worth, closer to Boca Raton market. Moderate supply, decent I-95 access. Popular with tenants serving south county without premium pricing.

Characteristics: Mixed class B and C industrial. Less congestion than WPB; good accessibility to south county markets.

Delray Beach (Higher-Cost Market)

Upscale market with limited large industrial supply. Industrial space here commands premium pricing. Serves Delray/Boca user base directly.

Characteristics: Limited availability in 10,000+ SF range. Mostly single-tenant or flex space; newer or highly maintained buildings.

Boca Raton (Premium Space)

South county premium submarket. Less industrial supply than west/north county; what exists is newer or highly functional. Serves Boca's high-end manufacturing and professional services.

Characteristics: Class A dominant. Limited direct availability; often requires new leases or repositionings. Premium market pricing.

Jupiter (Northern County)

Farthest north, closest to Stuart/Martin County. Emerging logistics nodes serving north-county and tri-county. Less congestion, newer supply.

Characteristics: Mix of class A and B space. Growing distribution presence; good accessibility to northern Florida markets.

Total Occupancy Cost for Large Warehouses

This is the make-or-break metric. Most business owners focus on base rent and miss the much larger NNN (triple net) burden. Here's how to calculate total occupancy cost:

Understanding Total Occupancy Cost

  • Base Rent: Core lease payment per square foot
  • Property Tax: County/municipal assessments
  • Insurance: Tenant/landlord liability and property coverage
  • CAM (Common Area Maintenance): Shared building and parking costs

Base rent is often only a portion of your total annual occupancy cost. The "three nets" (property tax, insurance, CAM) can add up to a significant amount on top of base rent.

Key drivers of total cost: Base rent, property tax (reassessment risk), insurance (hurricanes, reinsurance costs), CAM composition (parking, landscaping, building systems), building age and condition, and lease structure (NNN vs. gross vs. modified gross). Costs vary significantly by location, building quality, and market conditions. Always request detailed breakdowns from landlords and compare total occupancy cost across options, not just base rent.

Lease Structure: NNN vs Gross vs Modified Gross

Large warehouse leases come in three flavors. Understanding the difference is critical:

Structure Tenant Pays Cost Certainty Typical for 10,000+ SF
NNN (Triple Net) Base rent + property tax + insurance + CAM Low — costs vary annually Very common; landlord preferred
Gross Lease Single fixed rent (all-in) High — rent is fixed for lease term Rare for large industrial; usually in small spaces
Modified Gross Base rent + CAM only (taxes/insurance by landlord) Medium — some costs fixed, some variable Increasingly common; good compromise

Strategy: For large leases, push for modified gross or NNN with strict CAM caps (3-5% annual increases max) and base year stops for property taxes. This trades some cost predictability for landlord flexibility. See the NNN Lease Guide for detailed negotiation tactics.

Off-Market Access and Why It Matters for Large Space

A large share of industrial warehouse space in Palm Beach County never appears on public listing sites.

Why? Landlords of large multi-tenant buildings prefer dealing with known, creditworthy tenants and representatives they trust. They avoid public marketing costs and tenant screening complexity. Single-tenant buildings often stay off-market because the landlord has a longstanding relationship with a specific brokerage.

If you search only LoopNet, Zillow, or Google Maps, you're seeing only a fraction of what's available. Much of the inventory is in off-market channels managed by industrial brokers and Sales Associates with direct landlord relationships.

How to Access Off-Market Space

Work with a licensed Sales Associate who specializes in industrial and warehouse leasing in Palm Beach County. Your Sales Associate has:

  • Direct relationships with landlords and asset managers managing large portfolios
  • Early notice of availabilities before they hit the market
  • Market data and recent comparable lease terms
  • Negotiation experience and landlord relationship insights
  • Lease review expertise to protect your interests

Cost to you: Zero. Landlords pay the Sales Associate a commission (typically 4-6% of lease value). Tenant representation is free because the landlord bears the cost.

For large leases, off-market access and skilled negotiation can result in meaningful savings on rent and significantly better lease terms. This advantage compounds significantly over multi-year lease terms, making expert representation invaluable.

When to Start Your Large Warehouse Search

Timing impacts both availability and negotiating power. Here's the timeline:

6-12 Months Before Occupancy (Optimal)

Start your search this early. You get:

  • Access to confidential landlord pipelines of upcoming availabilities
  • Negotiating leverage (landlords aren't rushing to fill space)
  • Time to evaluate multiple options and run financial analyses
  • Space for lease drafting and architectural review
  • Zoning and municipal approval validation
  • Better terms on rent, tenant improvement allowances, and free rent periods

3-6 Months Before Occupancy (Moderate Pressure)

Still workable, but you're beginning to show urgency. Landlords know you're in active search mode. Negotiating leverage declines. On large deals, timing still favors early planning.

0-3 Months Before Occupancy (Emergency Mode)

Avoid if possible. You'll accept worse terms, pay higher rent, and make poor decisions under pressure. Landlords sense desperation and extract maximum value.

Why timing matters: The closer you get to your move date, the less negotiating leverage you have — landlords know a tight deadline limits your options, so starting early is one of your biggest advantages.

Key Negotiation Points for Large Warehouse Leases

For a 10,000+ SF lease, focus on these terms:

Base Rent Concessions

  • Free rent abatement: Negotiate 3-6 months free during buildout; reduces actual rent paid
  • Escalation caps: Cap annual rent increases at 2-3%, not unlimited market rates
  • Renewal options: Secure 2-3 renewal periods at fixed rates or % increases, not "fair market value"

CAM, Tax & Insurance Protections

  • CAM cap: Maximum 3-5% annual increases
  • Base year stops: Lock in year-1 property taxes and insurance; pay only increases above baseline
  • CAM exclusions: Major capital improvements (roof, HVAC, parking lot resurfacing) should not be passed to tenants
  • Audit rights: Retain the right to audit CAM, tax, and insurance charges annually

Tenant Improvement (TI) Allowance

  • Negotiate a tenant-improvement (TI) allowance sized to your buildout — on a large space it's a major cost lever
  • Negotiate this upfront; it reduces your out-of-pocket renovation costs
  • Clarify whether TI is "hard cost only" or includes design, permits, and contingencies

Operating Hours & Use Clauses

  • Ensure 24/7 operations are permitted if your business requires it
  • Verify no "quiet hours" or external parking restrictions that conflict with your operations
  • Confirm signage rights for branding (critical for large tenants)

Expansion and Contraction Rights

  • For long-term leases (5-10 years), secure the right to expand into adjacent space if available at market rates
  • For shorter leases, negotiate an exit clause if you downsize (e.g., reduce 20% of space after year 3)

Frequently Asked Questions — Large Warehouses

How do I find a large warehouse (10,000+ SF) in Palm Beach County? ▼

Public listing sites show only a portion of available large warehouse space. Much inventory is found through off-market channels managed by industrial brokers. Work with a licensed Sales Associate specializing in industrial leasing—they have direct landlord relationships and access to confidential availabilities. Tenant representation is free because landlords pay the commission. For best results, start your search 6-12 months before your occupancy date.

Should I lease, buy, or develop a large warehouse?

Lease if you need flexibility, want to preserve capital, or your occupancy timeline is uncertain. Buy if you plan to occupy 10+ years, want to build equity, and have access to financing. Develop if you own land or see long-term value creation potential. Model both scenarios: leasing typically wins for shorter holding periods (under 8 years), while buying becomes more attractive for longer terms (10+ years). Each situation is unique—compare total occupancy costs over your intended holding period.

What are the main submarkets for large warehouses in Palm Beach County? ▼

The key submarkets are West Palm Beach (logistics hub, highest supply, I-95 access), Riviera Beach/Mangonia Park (port access), Lake Worth/Lantana (mid-county, emerging supply), Boynton Beach (mid-county alternative), Delray Beach (higher-cost market), Boca Raton (premium space), and Jupiter (northern county). Each has distinct characteristics and cost profiles. Choose based on your customer base location, transportation access needs, building requirements, and budget.

What is total occupancy cost for a large warehouse? ▼

Total occupancy cost includes base rent, property taxes, insurance, and CAM. For a 20,000 SF space, base rent is typically one component, with the three net expenses (property tax, insurance, CAM) making up a substantial portion of total cost. Costs vary significantly by submarket (West Palm Beach, Riviera Beach, Delray, Boca Raton, etc.), building age and quality, and market conditions. Always request detailed cost breakdowns from landlords and calculate total annual occupancy cost across all options before making a decision. The three nets often represent 35-50% of your total occupancy expense.

What are the benefits of using a Sales Associate for a large warehouse search? ▼

A licensed Sales Associate provides access to off-market inventory, negotiates on your behalf, reviews leases for problematic terms, models total occupancy costs, and verifies zoning and environmental compliance. The cost is zero to you—landlords pay the commission. For a large deal, off-market access and expert negotiation often result in meaningful savings on rent and significantly improved lease terms. The savings compound substantially over multi-year lease periods, making expert representation highly valuable.

How far in advance should I start a large warehouse search? ▼

Start 6-12 months before your occupancy date. This timeline gives you access to off-market availabilities, time to negotiate favorable terms, and space for lease review, zoning verification, and due diligence. Landlords are more flexible with pricing and terms when you're not under immediate occupancy pressure. For every month closer to your occupancy date, your negotiating leverage drops as the deadline nears. Starting early is worth tens of thousands of dollars on large deals.

What's the difference between a NNN lease and a modified gross lease? ▼

In a NNN (triple net) lease, you pay base rent plus property taxes, insurance, and CAM—all separately. In a modified gross lease, you pay base rent plus CAM, but the landlord covers property taxes and insurance. For large warehouses, push for modified gross with a CAM cap (3-5% annual max) or NNN with strict CAM caps and base year stops. This gives you more cost predictability than pure NNN while preserving landlord flexibility. See the NNN Lease Guide for detailed negotiation strategies.
