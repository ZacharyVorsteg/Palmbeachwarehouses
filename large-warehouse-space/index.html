Why Work With a Tenant Rep

In commercial real estate, the landlord pays the commission — whether you have a rep or not. Going without one means giving up the only person at the table working for you.

Representation, Typically No Cost to You

On leases, the landlord pays the commission per the listing agreement. On most purchases, the seller pays both sides at closing. Either way, you usually get full representation without writing a check — I'll always confirm the structure up front on your specific deal.

Common Questions

Is this really free?
Yes. I'm a licensed sales associate (FL #SL3603483) at Cornerstone Realty — Cornerstone is the brokerage. In commercial real estate, the landlord pays the brokerage commission per the listing agreement they signed when the property went to market. If you walk in alone, the listing brokerage collects both sides. If I represent you, that same commission gets split between brokerages. Either way, you don't pay anything out of pocket.
What size and type of space is this for?
This is for large-format requirements — roughly 10,000 SF and up — across Palm Beach County. Any operation that needs scale: distribution, manufacturing, logistics/3PL, food & cold storage, contractors, e-commerce, marine, building supply, and more. If you need less than 10,000 SF, I cover that too — just start at my general search page.
What if I'm 6–12 months from my lease expiration?
That's the ideal time to start. Large blocks are scarce and big leases take months to source, tour, and negotiate. Reaching out early means you negotiate from leverage instead of under deadline pressure — no obligation to move until the right building shows up.
What happens after I submit the form?
I personally review every inquiry. You'll typically hear from me the same business day with a quick call or text. From there we'll schedule a 10–15 minute conversation to understand your needs in detail.
Do you only show your own listings?
No. As your tenant rep at Cornerstone Realty, my job is to find the best fit for your business — not to push my own inventory or Cornerstone's. I show every property in the market that matches, including listings from other brokerages.
What is CAM and why is it important?
CAM (Common Area Maintenance): Tenant's share of building operating costs (property taxes, insurance, parking lot maintenance, roof, lobby). On a $10/SF base rent lease, CAM often adds $2–4/SF, raising effective rent to $12–14/SF. Uncapped CAM can escalate 3–5% annually, hidden from base rent negotiations. On a 25,000 SF lease, a $2.50/SF CAM that escalates 3.5% annually costs an extra $60–120K over 10 years. I negotiate CAM caps at 2–2.5% annually or exclude certain costs.
What happens if I'm buying a building — is this still free?
Yes. As a buyer's agent, I represent you in the acquisition. On most industrial purchases in Palm Beach County, the seller pays both sides of the commission at closing — it's built into the sale price. I'll confirm the commission structure upfront. Your benefit: expert representation, off-market deal access, and professional negotiation at no cost to you.
What if I'm interested in land or a development site?
Land acquisition is my service too. I source off-market parcels, guide zoning and entitlement strategy, and help you evaluate build-to-suit potential. Commission structures vary on land (no standard split), so I'll discuss terms upfront. Focus is on finding the right parcel and positioning it for long-term development value.
What's the difference between a tenant rep and a landlord rep?
A tenant rep (or buyer rep) negotiates on behalf of you—the space-seeker. A landlord rep works for the property owner. Tenants work with a tenant rep to secure better lease rates, escalators, CAM caps, and TI allowances. The landlord's broker commission covers both sides per the listing agreement. Engaging a tenant rep is always recommended; you get professional representation at no cost.
How do I know if I should start looking now?
Start 6–12 months before current lease expires (or now, if you're exploring options). This timeline allows you to: (1) Survey market conditions and available space without pressure, (2) Negotiate from strength (landlord knows you're not desperate), (3) Execute 2–3 negotiation cycles, (4) Plan a smooth transition. If you start within 90 days of expiration, you lose all leverage and accept mediocre terms.
When should I start the search for large-format space?
Start 6–12 months before your lease expires, or now if you're exploring. Starting early lets you tour without pressure and negotiate from strength — within 90 days of expiration you lose leverage. I source large blocks continuously, including off-market, so starting today means you see options before the market does.
